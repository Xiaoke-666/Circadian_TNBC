# Circadian misalignment underlies immune escape in breast cancer
Circadian rhythms organize fundamental physiological programs across tissues and cell types. However, how circadian regulation structures cellular composition and molecular dynamics within the tumor microenvironment (TME), and how this temporal organization influences tumor immunity, remain unclear. Through temporal single-nucleus multiomic profiling of mouse breast tumors, we uncovered extensive circadian programs that are both cell-type–specific and shared across the TME, coordinating immune response, cell proliferation, RNA processing and metabolism. Notably, cancer epithelial cells exhibited global misalignment of circadian gene acrophase relative to immune populations. Functionally, this misalignment manifests as temporal decoupling between tumor proliferation and immune activation, discordance between antigen presentation and T cell recognition accompanied by intrinsic overlap of activation and exhaustion programs within T cells, and asynchronous PD-1/PD-L1 rhythms across tumor and immune compartments that sustain checkpoint-mediated suppression. We observed similar circadian misalignment in data from human triple-negative breast cancers (TNBC), supporting a conserved mechanism with potential implications for immunotherapy timing. Together, these findings establish intercellular circadian misalignment as a mechanism of tumor immune evasion and position the circadian architecture of the tumor–immune ecosystem as a previously underappreciated determinant of tumor development and therapeutic response.
